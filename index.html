I.

The state of play at the U

Miami has moved faster than most R1s on applied AI. The Frost Institute seeded four generative AI grants in the last cycle across law, ocean sciences, physics, and ophthalmology. The Miller School stood up the Office of AI in Medical Education and launched MILADS for translational research. Herbert has an AI minor at the undergraduate level and an MS in Business Technology at the graduate level.

Each of these is real. Each of them also lives inside its own school, with its own tooling, its own context, and its own relationship to the Center for Data Ethics and Society. The question for 2026 is whether those efforts compose into a coherent university-wide capability or stay parallel.

  • Frost Institute for Data Science and Computing

    Four GenAI grants, four schools

    Law, ocean sciences, physics, ophthalmology. Cross-school seeding from a central hub.

  • Miller School of Medicine

    Office of AI in Medical Education

    Stood up in 2025. First cohort through the new AI-in-medicine course. First-year mandate, playbook still forming.

  • Miller School of Medicine

    MILADS translational platform

    Agentic data-scientist platform, live for research use. Context hand-off to the model is still ad hoc across PIs.

  • Herbert Business School

    AI in the curriculum, both levels

    Minor in AI for Business Technology. MS in Business Technology with AI emphasis. Executive education next.

II.

The Eduba frame

Eduba works with universities and research groups on the methodology layer. The operating view is that about 60% of what teams think of as AI problems are traditional code and database work, another 30% are rule-based logic, and roughly 10% are genuine AI problems.

Applied to Miami: MILADS doing retrieval over clinical study data is partly AI, but the structure of the study protocol, the versioning of the data dictionary, and the audit trail for ethics review are all traditional or rule-based. Sorting those layers before the model runs is the difference between a platform researchers keep using and one they quietly abandon.

IV.

The paper the conversation routes through

ACM TiiS Interpretable Context Methodology

Folder structure as agent architecture.

The paper argues that agent context organised as a layered filesystem (L0 identity, L1 doctrine, L2 standard operating procedure, L3 factory, L4 working artifacts) gives agent systems measurable interpretability and reproducibility. It is the methodology the rest of Eduba's work runs on top of. The reason it travels well inside an AAU research university: it is itself academically positioned, peer-reviewable, reproducible, and written for faculty readers.

A second relevant paper, the Ethics Engine. A psychometric assessment tool for evaluating ideological and moral patterns in LLMs. Cited here because the Center for Data Ethics and Society inside IDSC is the natural first reader for this line of work.

V.

Five questions worth discussing

  1. 01

    When a Miller clinical team, an IDSC researcher, and a Herbert faculty member all want to stand up a GenAI workflow on the same underlying data, who draws the governance boundary and where does that conversation live.

  2. 02

    For MILADS, how are researchers handing the platform their study protocol context today. Is there a shared pattern across studies or is each PI starting from scratch.

  3. 03

    Of the four 2024 to 2025 IDSC GenAI grant projects, which one is furthest along, and what did the team learn about where the AI actually added lift versus where it was noise.

  4. 04

    Where does the Center for Data Ethics and Society sit in the project lifecycle: before, during, or after deployment.

  5. 05

    If the Office of AI in Medical Education had a playbook for structuring clinical AI context in a reproducible way, would that plug into the existing curriculum or would it need a new course.
